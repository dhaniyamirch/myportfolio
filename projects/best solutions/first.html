Scandium is a soft, silvery transition element which occurs in rare minerals from Scandinavia

Titanium(Ti)

William Gregor

It's a silvery-white metal that's lightweight, strong, and corrosion-resistant. Titanium is the 9th most abundant element on Earth and is commonly found in rocks, clay, and sand.

Vanadium(V)

Andrés Manuel del Río

It's a hard, silvery-white, ductile metal. Vanadium is rarely found in nature, but it can be isolated artificially.

Chromium(Cr)

Vaughlin

Chromium metal is valued for its high corrosion resistance and hardness. A major development in steel production was the discovery that steel could be made highly resistant to corrosion and discoloration by adding metallic chromium to form stainless steel.

Mangenese(Mn)

Carl Wilhelm Scheele

It is a hard, brittle, silvery metal that is often found in minerals with iron. Manganese is a transition metal that is solid at room temperature. It has many industrial alloy uses, especially in stainless steels.

Iron(Fe)

The Hittites of ancient Egypt

Iron is a chemical element with atomic number 26 and symbol Fe. It is a metal that belongs to the periodic table's first transition series and group 8. It is the most common element on Earth by mass, forming much of the outer and inner core of Earth.

Cobalt(Co)

George Brandt

Cobalt is a transition metal. It's found in the Earth's crust in a chemically combined form, except for small deposits in alloys of natural meteoric iron. The free element is a hard, lustrous, silvery metal

Nickel(Ni)

Axel Fredrik Cronstedt

It's a chemical element with the symbol Ni. Nickel is a hard, ductile, transition metal that's silvery-white with a slight golden tinge. It's similar to iron in strength and toughness, but more like copper in resistance to corrosion and oxidation. Nickel has high electrical and thermal conductivity.

Copper(Cu)

Known since ancient times

It is a soft, malleable, and ductile metal with very high thermal and electrical conductivity.

zinc(zn)

Known since ancient times

Zinc is a chemical element with the symbol Zn. It's a transition metal that's solid at room temperature. Zinc is brittle and has a shiny-greyish appearance when oxidation is removed.

Yttrium(Y)

Johan Gadolin

It is a silvery-metallic transition metal chemically similar to the lanthanides and has often been classified as a "rare-earth element".

zicronium(zr)

Martin Heinrich Klaproth

The name zirconium comes from the mineral zircon, which is the most important source of zirconium. The word zirconium is related to the Persian word zargun which means "gold-like" or "as gold".

Niobium(Nb)

Charles Hatchett

Niobium has an atomic number of 41. It's a transition metal with the symbol Nb and an atomic mass of 92.91.

Molybdenum(Mo)

Carl Welhelm Scheele

Molybdenum is an essential trace mineral that occurs naturally in foods and is available in supplements. It is a component of four different enzymes in the body that help break down proteins, alcohol, drugs, and toxins. Molybdenum-containing enzymes also break down purines and sulfites.

Technetium(Tc)

Carlo Perrier Emilio Segrè

It is the lightest element whose isotopes are all radioactive. All available technetium is produced as a synthetic element.

Ruthenium(Ru)

It is a rare transition metal belonging to the platinum group of the periodic table. Like the other metals of the platinum group, ruthenium is inert to most other chemicals

Palladium(Pd)

William Hyde Wollaston

Palladium was discovered in 1803 by English chemist William Hyde Wollaston. It's a member of the nickel group of elements and the platinum group of metals. Other elements in the transition metal group include: Gold, Iron, Silver, Platinum

Silver(Ag)

Known since ancient times

A soft, white, lustrous transition metal, it exhibits the highest electrical conductivity, thermal conductivity, and reflectivity of any metal.

Cadmium(Cd)

Friedrich Strohmeyer

Cadmium is a natural element in the earth's crust. It's usually found as a mineral combined with other elements such as oxygen, chlorine, or sulfur

Lanthanide(La)

the elements with atomic numbers from 58 to 71, following the element lanthanum. They are called rare earth metals since the occurrence of these elements is very small (3×10-4 % of the Earth's crust).

Hafnium(Hf)

George Charles de Hevesy and Dirk Coster

A shiny, silvery metal that resists corrosion and can be drawn into wires. Hafnium is a good absorber of neutrons and is used to make control rods, such as those found in nuclear submarines. It also has a very high melting point and because of this is used in plasma welding torches.

Tantalum(Ta)

Anders Gustaf Ekeberg

It always takes a valence of 5, so, for example, its oxide contains two atoms of tantalum and five of oxygen. In its pure state, tantalum is a grey-blue metal, which can be polished to a silvery sheen.

Tungsten(W)

William D. Coolidge

Tungsten is a rare, lustrous, greyish-white metal. It has the highest melting point and lowest vapor pressure of all metals. Tungsten is also very dense and has excellent corrosion resistance.

Rhenium(Re)

Walter Noddack, Ida Tacke and Otto Berg

Rhenium is among the rarest metals on Earth. It does not occur uncombined in nature or as a compound in a mineable mineral species. It is, however, widely spread throughout the Earth's crust to the extent of about 0.001 parts per million.

Opium(os)

Smithson Tennant

Osmium tetroxide is highly toxic. It can cause chemical burns to the skin, eyes, and respiratory tract. Acute inhalation exposure can lead to a burning sensation, tearing, cough, headache, wheezing, shortness of breath, pulmonary edema, and, ultimately, death at high concentrations.

Iridium(Ir)

Dr. Raymond J. Leopold

Iridium is a hard, silvery metal. It is almost as unreactive as gold. It has a very high density and melting point. Iridium is the most corrosion-resistant material known.

Platinum(Pt)

Antonio de Ulloa

Platinum is a chemical element with the symbol Pt and atomic number 78. It's a dense, malleable, ductile, and highly unreactive transition metal. Platinum is also one of the purest precious metals, typically being 95% pure.

Gold(Au)

An Egyptian alchemist named Zosimos was the first to find pure gold

Gold is a chemical element; it has symbol Au and atomic number 79. In pure form, it is a bright, slightly orange-yellow, dense, soft, malleable, and ductile metal. Chemically, gold is a transition metal, a group 11 element, and one of the noble metals.

Mercury(Hg)

Daniel Gabriel Fahrenheit

Mercury is a naturally-occurring chemical element found in rock in the earth's crust, including in deposits of coal. On the periodic table, it has the symbol "Hg" and its atomic number is 80. It exists in several forms: Elemental (metallic) mercury.

Actnium(Ac)

André-Louis Debierne

Actinium is a soft, silvery-white radioactive metal. It glows blue in the dark because its intense radioactivity excites the air around it. Actinium is a very powerful source of alpha rays, but is rarely used outside research. Actinium has no known biological role.

rutherfordium(Rf)

Ernest Rutherford

Rutherfordium is a synthetic chemical element. Its official chemical symbol is Rf, and its atomic number is 104, which means that a rutherfordium atom has 104 protons in its nucleus. Rutherfordium was the first super-heavy element to be discovered.

Seborgium(Sg)

Albert Ghiorso and Glenn T. Seaborg

Seaborgium is not found in nature. It can only be prepared through reactions in a particle accelerator, such as a cyclotron. For example, bombarding lead-208 with chromium-54 yields seaborgium-260. The most stable isotope of seaborgium is Sg 271, which has a half life of 2.4 minutes.

Bhorium(Bh)

Bohrium is a synthetic element, meaning it is not a naturally-occurring element. Several isotopes of Bohrium have been discovered, the most stable of which is bohrium-270, with a half-life of just 61 seconds.
